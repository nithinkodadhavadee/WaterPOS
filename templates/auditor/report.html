A. Business Impacts:

Water risks transcend industry sectors, affecting operational resilience and sustainability. The WEF's recognition of water crises as a top global risk highlights the imperative for businesses to address water-related challenges proactively.

B. Conservation Strategies:

Effective water management begins with monitoring and mapping water consumption across various processes. Regular water audits play a crucial role in quantifying water usage and losses, enabling businesses to diagnose challenges and implement optimal solutions.

V. CGWA guidelines:

As per the notification dated 24/09/2020 from CGWA, all industries abstracting groundwater exceeding 100 m3/day must conduct an annual water audit through auditors certified by CII/FICCI/NPC/PHDCCI. Following completion, they are required to submit their water audit reports to CGWA within three months.

Water audit help industries, hospitals and commercial establishments to adhere to CGWA and state pollution control board guideline mandates and adhere to compliances.

The Central Groundwater Board (CGWB) oversees groundwater quality and quantity, lacking authority to charge for industrial groundwater use. The Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB) and State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs) regulate industrial water pollution but lack control over water sourcing. This gap hinders water conservation and pollution control efforts. While some industries implement zero-discharge projects, others discharge untreated effluents. Experts advocate for self-regulation and best practices dissemination by industry associations like FICCI, CII, and ASSOCHAM. A robust industrial water policy must empower regulatory bodies, promote collaboration, and incentivize conservation to ensure sustainable water management in India's industrial sector.

1.1 What is water audit and why is it important?

Water audit is a fundamental process aimed at assessing and managing water loss within distribution systems, encompassing variables such as seepage, evaporation, leakage, and unauthorized withdrawals. It enhances comprehension of water utilization post-source while facilitating meticulous documentation of distribution systems and user profiles. This process optimizes resource management, bolstering reliability and efficiency. By diagnosing underlying issues and proposing tailored solutions, water audit yields reductions in water loss, augments financial performance, and fortifies supply system reliability. Additionally, it fosters enhanced public health and property safeguards, improves public relations, and mitigates legal liabilities and disruptions. Aligned with the UN Sustainable Development Goal 6, "Clean Water and Sanitation," water audit underscores the imperative for businesses to prioritize water conservation amid escalating global scarcity. By recognizing the interconnectedness of water availability, business operations, and global water challenges, water audit advocates for sustainable practices to alleviate adverse impacts on communities and ecosystems. In essence, water audit serves as an indispensable tool for evaluating performance, identifying inefficiencies, and steering future system enhancements.

2. Scope of Work

The scope of this water audit encompasses several key areas aimed at assessing, monitoring, and improving the water supply and usage system of {company name}. By evaluating the entire water management system, a water audit identifies opportunities for conservation, efficiency improvements, and sustainability measures. The scope of work is listed in detail below:

2.1. Water Supply System Assessment

A thorough evaluation of the water supply system includes:

  • Status of Water Supply Components: Assessing the condition and functionality of various components within the water supply network, such as pipes, pumps, valves, and fittings.
  • Source Identification: Documenting the types of water sources, including municipal supply, groundwater, surface water, and rainwater harvesting systems.
  • Water Flow and Metering Devices: Evaluating the presence, accuracy, and condition of water flow meters and gauging devices to ensure accurate measurement of water input and output volumes.

2.2. Water Utilization Analysis

Understanding the patterns and efficiency of water use within the facility is crucial. This includes:

  • Consumption Patterns: Mapping out the water usage across different sections of the establishment, such as production areas, administrative buildings, canteens, and washrooms etc
  • Water Balance Analysis: Calculating the input and output volumes and mapping out the water distribution across various points of {company name} to create a comprehensive water balance diagram, highlighting areas of high consumption and potential inefficiencies. First the baseline water balance chart and diagram is laid out and then after finalizing the water conservation methods an ideal water balance chart and diagram is established.
  • Specific Water Use and Conservation: Identifying specific water use points and potential conservation opportunities to reduce overall consumption.

2.3. Wastewater Management

Proper management of wastewater is essential for sustainability and regulatory compliance:

  • Generation and Treatment: Identifying the sources and volumes of wastewater generated from all utilities and processes. Evaluating the existing wastewater treatment facilities i.e. ETP/STP for their efficiency and effectiveness.
  • Recycling and Reuse: First investigating the current usage point of treated wastewater across the facility from both Effluent treatment plant and sewage treatment plant. Further investigating additional opportunities for recycling and reusing treated wastewater within the facility. This includes assessing the feasibility of using treated wastewater for irrigation, cooling, or other non-potable applications.

2.4. System Losses and Leak Detection

Detecting and mitigating water losses within the system is a critical component of a water audit:

  • Leakage Identification: Using monitoring devices such as pressure and flow meters to detect leaks and inefficiencies in the water distribution network.
  • System Efficiency: Evaluating the operational efficiency of water-using systems and recommending upgrades or repairs to reduce losses.

2.5. Water Quality Assessment

Ensuring the quality of water at various usage points is vital for health and operational efficiency:

  • Water Quality Monitoring: Regular monitoring of water quality at strategic points within the distribution system to detect contaminants and ensure compliance with health standards.
  • Quality Requirements Mapping: Mapping out the water quality requirements for different processes and user areas to ensure that the water supplied meets the necessary standards.

2.6. Rainwater Harvesting and Groundwater Recharge

Incorporating sustainable water sources is a key aspect of modern water management:

  • Rainwater Harvesting: Evaluating existing rainwater harvesting systems and their efficiency. Identifying opportunities for enhancing rainwater capture and utilization.
  • Groundwater Recharge: Studying the current recharge structures and their efficiency first. Then implementing additional artificial groundwater recharge techniques plan to improve the sustainability of water sources and reduce dependency on external supplies.

2.7. Cost Analysis

A detailed financial analysis helps in understanding the economic impact of water usage and conservation measures:

  • Cost of Water Supply: Calculating the total expenditure on water supply, including operation and maintenance costs.
  • Financial Savings: Identifying potential cost savings through water conservation measures, improved efficiency, and reduced wastage.

2.8. Technological Recommendations

Adopting new technologies can significantly enhance water management:

  • Advanced Metering Infrastructure (AMI): Recommending the adoption of AMI systems for real-time data collection and improved resource management.
  • Energy and Water Fixtures: Assessing the performance of existing water circulating pumps and motors, and recommending energy-efficient fixtures.

2.9. Comprehensive Water Audit Report

The final audit report should provide a detailed overview of the findings, recommendations, and actionable insights:

  • Consumption and Wastewater Patterns: Documenting the water consumption and wastewater generation patterns within the facility.
  • Water Balance and Mapping: Presenting a complete water balance diagram and mapping out water quality requirements.
  • Opportunities for Conservation: Highlighting specific opportunities for water conservation and the methods for implementing these measures.
  • Financial Analysis: Providing a thorough financial analysis, including investment requirements and potential savings.
  • Schematic Diagrams: Including detailed schematic diagrams of the water distribution and wastewater treatment systems.
